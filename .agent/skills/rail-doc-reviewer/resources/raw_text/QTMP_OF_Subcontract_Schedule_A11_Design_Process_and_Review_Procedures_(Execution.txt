SCHEDULE A11
Design Process and Review Procedures
Part 1 - Design Development Requirements
1. GENERAL
1.1 Design Development Requirements
This Part 1 sets out the Design Development Requirements for the Design Development
Process.
1.2 Related provisions
(a) These Design Development Requirements should be read in conjunction with the
other parts of this deed that relate to the development of the Design Documentation,
including in particular, the following:
(i) clause 21 (Design) of the deed;
(ii) Part 2 of this Schedule A11 (Design Process and Review Procedures); and
(iii) the Design Management Plan.
1.2A The Contractor’s role in the Design Development Process
(a) This paragraph 1.2A applies notwithstanding any provision to the contrary in this
Schedule A11 (Design Process and Review Procedures), and no provision in this
Schedule A11 (Design Process and Review Procedures) in any way limits the
application of this paragraph 1.2A.
(b) The parties must work together collaboratively during the Design Development
Process in respect of the Design Documentation including in particular, prior to the
submission of ‘Submitted Documents’ (as defined in the Project Deed) to the
Reviewing Parties under the Project Deed.
(c) Without limiting paragraph 1.2A(b), the Builder must:
(i) consult and liaise with the Contractor during the preparation of the Design
Documentation;
(ii) do all things reasonably necessary to assist the Contractor in observing the
Design Development Process and the ‘Design Development Process’ under the
Project Deed (including any ancillary and informal design processes); and
(iii) actively involve the Contractor in the Design Development Process (including
any ancillary and informal design processes); and
(iv) allow the Contractor to review all Design Documentation,
so that the Contractor is able to reasonably form the view (from such consultation,
liaison and progressive review of the Design Documentation ahead of submission to
the Reviewing Parties) that the Design Documentation complies (in terms of format
and substance) with the requirements of this deed.

1.6 Detailed Design Review (DDR)
The Builder acknowledges and agrees that:
(a) the purpose of the DDR is to ensure that the design has been translated into
constructible / deliverable design, and will meet all test and acceptance / completion
criteria that the Builder will need to satisfy;
(b) during the DDR Design Stage, the Builder must submit for Review Design
Documentation of sufficient detail to ensure that the design complies with the
requirements of the Builder Requirements (Ormeau Facility) and can be constructed

evidence that demonstrates that the detailed design achieves all the requirements

Documentation sufficient to achieve the requirements of the relevant Authorities and
third parties;

manufacturing drawings for the Works, as defined in the Design Management
Plan;

DDR stage, highlighting changes from the PDR stage;
(iii) the Requirement Verification Matrix updated to provide traceability to the
design evidence for every requirement;

the design evidence for every SEMS Requirement;

and derived requirements under the Builder Requirements (Ormeau Facility)
and SEMS;

testing, commissioning and acceptance / completion criteria for the Works;

markings, typical details & sections, carpark lighting details;
(c) all facilities should be accompanied with detail drawings clearly identifying
dimensions and content locations such as emergency equipment, facades, HVAC
(Heating, Ventilating and Air Conditioning);
(d) transponder allocation diagrams should include locations for Automatic Train
Protection transponder (ATP) groups;
(e) working plan & section (WP&S) drawings – should include civil and track aspects,
track geometry (Vertical and horizontal), culverts, bridges, tunnels, stations, roads,
access tracks, fencing and pedestrian crossings. These drawings are used for
interpreting track geometry (curves, straights, diamonds, turnouts, loops) and to
derive turnout information, track cant and design speed. WP&S drawings consist of:
(i) control line set-out tables: these are tables containing alignment de-tails for
the various control lines (tracks), rail cross sections, platform clearances,
general arrangement layouts, formation cross sections, culvert details and
sections, earthworks and drainage details, general details gantry and
crossovers/turnouts. The assets include turn-outs, toe of switch (TOS)
height/level, type, angle, rail size, track curves/spiral/tangents, radius (also
provided in the 12D extract), design speed, cant and grade;
(ii) line diagrams (staging),working plan & section (drawings) based on chainage.
Each drawing represents a section of the project, and depicts that section as
a plan view with a corresponding longitudinal section of the track centreline
(control line). Details shown include track grades (vertical geometry -
gradients), track curves, spirals, tangents (horizontal geometry), rail levels,
formation levels, bridge clearances and kilometre (chainage). The AutoCAD
DWG format should be exported to a spreadsheet to combine the curves and
straights feature classes curve segments, spiral segments and tangent
segments.
(iii) electrical/isolation diagram - also known as the isolation diagram includes
details of power supply cubicles (PSC), transformers (auto-transformers,
auxiliary supply transformers, booster, power transfer, TSC, TCU, etc.),

1.2 Survey/GIS Locations
Asset locations are derived in either of the following ways:
(a) direct from 12D – for track geometry;
(b) via linear referencing (such as ATP, track circuits);
(c) from Survey – co-ordinates in the local project projection are provided by the
Program or Project survey team for the assets in question. In the majority of cases,
the survey team will do this as part of the construction verification process;
(d) from DGPS – co-ordinates in the Geographical Coordinate System, GDA94 (or
GDA2020) are provided by the Program GIS Team using a Differential GPS (DGPS)
Unit for the assets in question, enabling field co-ordinates to be captured at sub-
metre accuracy. This is typically done in the absence of data from the survey team;
(e) through manually creation by overlaying imagery or as-built set out plans from the
survey team, such as concrete foundations for masts or transformers.
For all assets, the GIS coordinates must be collected, incorporating the 3 axis measurements, and
these data sets should have the Metadata information saved with them. The Metadata is used to
determine the accuracy and validity of the da-ta collection method used. For example the location
accuracy required to specify the position of equipment hut is not critical, however the centreline
data of a track position is critical and therefore the metadata file should specify the accuracy of the
data collection method to within centimetres.
